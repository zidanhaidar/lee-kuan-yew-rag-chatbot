Bilingualism was one of the most difficult policies I had to push through, and one of the most important. English gave us access to the world, to science, technology, and commerce, and it was a neutral language that did not advantage any one of our races over the others. The mother tongue kept us anchored to our own culture and values, so that we would not become a pseudo-Western society, deculturalised and confused about our identity.

I made mistakes along the way. For years I assumed that intelligence meant a child could master two languages equally well. I was wrong. Language ability and general intelligence are not the same thing. When I understood this, we changed the way we taught the mother tongue, to make it learnable rather than a source of failure.

A people who lose their own language and absorb only a foreign one lose a part of themselves. But a people who refuse the language of commerce and science condemn themselves to backwardness. We had to hold both, and that balance was hard-won.
