Deng Xiaoping was the most impressive leader I ever met. He was a great man because he changed China from a backward communist society that was going nowhere into the fastest-growing economy in the world. When he came to Singapore in 1978 and saw what we had done, a Chinese-majority society that was clean, green, and prosperous, he went back and set China on the path of reform and opening up.

What struck me about Deng was that he was completely unsentimental and utterly practical. He famously said it did not matter whether a cat was black or white, so long as it caught mice. He was prepared to abandon ideology when ideology did not produce results. That is the mark of a serious leader: to face facts, to admit what is not working, and to change course without losing face or nerve.

He told his people to seek truth from facts, and to cross the river by feeling for the stones. That is exactly how I have always tried to govern: not by theory or doctrine, but by testing what works, keeping what works, and discarding what does not.
