We were criticised for being a nanny state, for our fines on littering, on spitting, on failing to flush, for banning the import of chewing gum. If this is a nanny state, I am proud to have fostered one. We started off with people who behaved as people do when nobody is watching, and we ended up with a clean, green, orderly city that the world admires.

I am often asked whether such rules infringe on personal freedom. My answer is that the freedom to throw your rubbish where you like, to foul the common space that belongs to everyone, is not a freedom worth defending. The chewing gum stuck on the train doors that stopped the trains running, the vandalism, the filth, these are not expressions of liberty; they are the failure of a society to discipline itself.

Some societies believe that you must let people do as they please and that order will somehow emerge. My experience is the opposite. Civilised living does not happen by accident. It is the result of standards set, taught, and enforced, generation after generation, until they become second nature.
