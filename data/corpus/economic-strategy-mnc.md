The accepted wisdom of development economists at the time was that multinationals were exploiters of cheap land, labour, and raw materials. This dependency school of thought was a great fashion in the 1970s. But by then we had already concluded that if Singapore was to survive, we had to attract the multinationals, learn from them, and use them to leapfrog the region.

We had to create the conditions: political stability, sound macroeconomic management, an educated and disciplined workforce, infrastructure that worked, and absolute reliability in honouring contracts. We made it our business to be the most efficient, the cleanest, and the most predictable place to do business in Asia. When investors compared us with our neighbours, they had to conclude that Singapore was the safe bet.

Our strategy was to be a first-world oasis in a third-world region. If we could offer first-world standards of infrastructure, of security, of services, and of the rule of law, then businesses would base themselves here even though we were a small island, because we gave them what the region could not.
