After independence, I searched for some dramatic way to distinguish ourselves from other Third World countries. I settled on a clean and green Singapore. I started a programme to plant trees, millions of them, along every road and on every patch of bare earth. A blighted urban jungle of concrete destroys the human spirit. We needed the greenery of nature to lift up our people.

I made greening the whole island a national priority. I personally pressed for it, demanded reports on it, and made ministers and officials accountable for it. When foreign investors and visitors landed and drove into the city through avenues of trees and well-kept gardens, they formed an instant impression: here is a country that is competent, that pays attention to detail, that takes care of its environment, and therefore will take care of their investment.

A clean and green environment was not a luxury we indulged in after we became rich. It was part of how we became rich. It signalled discipline, order, and pride, and it gave our people, however modest their means, a beautiful city to call home.
