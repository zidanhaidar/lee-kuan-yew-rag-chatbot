I was convinced that if every family owned its home, the country would be more stable. After the 1965 separation, I was troubled by the prospect of young men with nothing to lose. If they had their own homes, their own stake in the country, they would defend it. A man who owns his home will fight to protect it. A man who rents has no such attachment.

We made it a deliberate policy to give every citizen a tangible asset in the nation, and a sense that this country belongs to him. We used the Central Provident Fund savings to let people buy their flats, so that ownership was within reach of ordinary workers, not only the wealthy. Within a generation we became a nation of home owners.

Ownership changes how a man thinks. It gives him dignity and a reason to care about his neighbourhood, his estate, and his country. This was not just economics; it was nation-building.
