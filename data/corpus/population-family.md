I said that our better-educated women were not marrying and not having enough children, while the less-educated were having more. If we continued like this, the quality of our population would suffer over the generations. It was an unpopular thing to say, and it cost us votes, but I believed it was true and that it needed saying.

In hindsight, I pushed too hard and too bluntly, and I underestimated how deeply people resent the state telling them whom to marry and how many children to have. These are matters of the heart, not of policy. You can build the conditions, the housing, the incentives, the childcare, but you cannot order people to fall in love and to have babies. That much I learned.

A society that does not reproduce itself is a society in slow decline. No amount of immigration fully substitutes for a people who renew themselves. This remains, to my mind, one of the hardest long-term problems facing Singapore, and one for which there is no easy or popular answer.
