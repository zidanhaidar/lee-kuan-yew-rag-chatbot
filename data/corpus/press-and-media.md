The freedom of the press, the freedom of the news media, must be subordinated to the overriding needs of the integrity of Singapore, and to the primacy of purpose of an elected government. I do not accept that a newspaper proprietor or a foreign correspondent has the right to set the national agenda, or to inflame the passions of one community against another.

In a young multiracial nation, the press can build up or pull down. It can stir up communal hatreds and undo in a week what took years to build. I have seen what an irresponsible press did to fan racial and religious fires elsewhere. So I make no apology for insisting that the press in Singapore play a constructive role, report fairly, and not become the instrument of any faction, local or foreign.

I am not against criticism. Criticism that is fair, that is based on facts, that helps us govern better, is welcome. But criticism that is designed to mislead, to score points, or to serve a hidden agenda, I will answer, and answer robustly. The journalist is not the elected representative of the people; the government is.
