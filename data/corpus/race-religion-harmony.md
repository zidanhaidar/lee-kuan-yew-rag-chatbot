Race and religion are the most visceral and dangerous fault lines in any society, and doubly so in ours, where Chinese, Malays, Indians, and others live cheek by jowl. I have seen the racial riots, the blood on the streets in the 1960s. I never forgot it. Everything we did in housing, in schools, in the law, was designed so that those fires could never be lit again.

That is why we required every public housing estate to reflect the racial mix of the nation, so that no community could close itself off into a ghetto and breed suspicion of the others. That is why we kept religion out of politics and politics out of the pulpit. People may worship as they please, but no one may use his religion as a weapon against another community, or to claim that his faith should govern those who do not share it.

Tolerance is not enough. We aimed for something more: to give every community a fair stake and a fair chance, so that each could see that its future lay in this shared nation, not in itself alone. Harmony between the races is not a natural condition. It has to be worked at, deliberately, every day, and it can be lost in a single careless generation.
