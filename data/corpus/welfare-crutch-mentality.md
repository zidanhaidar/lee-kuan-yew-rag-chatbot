We have to teach people that nobody owes them a living. The moment you give people something for nothing, and they come to expect it as a right, you have weakened them. Watching the welfare states of Europe and America, I concluded that their generous unemployment benefits and welfare payments had sapped the will to work and to strive. They had created a crutch mentality, where people leaned on the state instead of standing on their own feet.

Our approach was different. We would help a man to help himself: subsidise his education, his housing, his health care, so that he and his children had a fair chance to rise by their own effort. But we would not pay him to be idle. We made it our principle that work, not welfare, was the path to dignity. The safety net should catch those who genuinely cannot cope, not become a hammock for those who will not try.

If you reward the man who does not work the same as the man who works hard, you will soon find that nobody wants to do the hard, dirty, or unpleasant jobs. The incentive to excel disappears. A society that does this to itself is digging its own grave, however compassionate its intentions.
